# BIZEE Product-Centralized Operating Model

## Summary

BIZEE's product-centralized operating model (v1.0, January 2026) defines how the organization transitions to a product-led culture where Product Managers own end-to-end product domains with full accountability for vision, strategy, and outcomes. The model establishes three interconnected layers—Strategic, Tactical, and Execution—with clear roles, governance ceremonies, and processes that enable autonomous squads to deliver value while maintaining strategic alignment. A critical Solution Design layer ensures architectural coherence and platform scalability across all initiatives.

## Details

## Overview

This operating model defines how BIZEE will transition to a product-led culture where Product Managers have end-to-end ownership of their domains, with full ownership and accountability for product vision, strategy, and outcomes.[1] The framework establishes the governance structures, processes, and decision rights that enable autonomous squads to deliver exceptional value while maintaining strategic alignment and operational excellence.

## Product-Led Vision

The organization's vision centers on five key pillars:[2]

- Product Managers drive strategic direction with deep customer insight and market understanding
- Cross-functional squads operate with autonomy and clear accountability
- Decision-making happens closest to the customer and product
- Continuous learning and adaptation are embedded in daily operations
- Success is measured by outcomes and customer value, not output and activities

## Core Principles

**Ownership & Accountability:** Product Managers own the end-to-end product lifecycle from vision to delivery to outcomes, with authority to make decisions within their domain.[3]

**Autonomy with Alignment:** Squads have autonomy to determine how they achieve objectives while remaining aligned to company strategy through clear OKRs, shared frameworks, and transparent communication.[4]

**Cross-Functional Collaboration:** Success requires seamless collaboration across squads, with Program Managers facilitating coordination and dependency management across the product portfolio.[5]

**Outcome Orientation:** Success is measured by customer and business outcomes, not by features shipped. Every initiative must tie back to measurable impact.[6]

**Continuous Improvement:** The organization embraces experimentation, learns from data, and iterates rapidly. The operating model itself will evolve based on what is learned.[7]

## Three-Layer Operating Model

The operating model consists of three interconnected layers:[8]

**Strategic Layer** (Leadership Team, PMO): Define company OKRs and strategic priorities, approve resource allocation, set domain direction, and establish the operating model governance, planning cadence, standards, and performance tracking.

**Tactical Layer** (Domain Leaders, Program Managers, PMO): Translate OKRs and domain strategy into quarterly plans and roadmaps, prioritize initiatives, manage dependencies and sequencing, confirm resourcing and capacity plans, define measurable outcomes, and govern delivery through reviews, risk management, and escalation paths.

**Execution Layer** (Product Managers, Squads, Engineering Teams, Operations Teams): Deliver committed work through sprints and execution plans, complete defined milestones, manage day-to-day task coordination, maintain quality standards, resolve blockers, provide progress reporting, and ensure outputs meet acceptance criteria and contribute to expected outcomes.

## Solution Design Integration

A critical Solution Design layer ensures all initiatives—whether business-driven or technical—are architected for long-term platform coherence, scalability, and AI safety before execution begins.[9]

Without architectural governance, autonomous squads can inadvertently create fragmented data models, inconsistent user experiences, technical debt, security gaps, and duplicated capabilities.[10] Solution Design operates as a routing and validation layer between product definition and squad execution, with three approval tracks:[11]

- **Fast Track:** Standard patterns, minimal complexity, approved within 1-2 days
- **Standard Review:** Moderate complexity, 3-5 day review cycle
- **Deep Design:** Novel architecture, significant platform impact, 1-2 week design sprint

Solution Design-approved items are prerequisites before items move to "Now" (0-3 months) in backlog refinement and before commitment to sprints.[12]

## Key Roles & Responsibilities

**Product Manager:** Owns product vision, strategy, roadmap, success metrics, end-to-end backlog, backlog grooming, prioritization, and initiative definition. Conducts customer discovery, market analysis, stakeholder management, squad leadership, go-to-market collaboration, and data-driven decision making. Has full authority over feature prioritization, ownership of what gets built and when, decision-making on scope trade-offs, and authority to adjust roadmap based on learnings.[13]

**Squad Lead:** Owns domain strategy, squad delivery capability, resource allocation across products within the domain, strategic alignment to company objectives, and backlog attribution. Removes blockers, facilitates collaboration between Product Managers, represents domain in leadership forums, and drives hiring and team development. Has final authority on domain resource allocation, approval of major strategic pivots, and hiring decisions.[14]

**Program Manager:** Manages cross-product dependencies, coordinates delivery timelines, identifies risks and blockers, and drives cross-squad alignment. Facilitates dependency mapping sessions, runs cross-squad standups and planning meetings, maintains integrated delivery timelines, escalates conflicts, coordinates communication, and supports release planning. Does not own product decisions but acts as connective tissue between Product Managers.[15]

**PMO (Program Management Office):** Owns initiative and project portfolio, defines prioritization frameworks, tracks progress and health, and facilitates portfolio governance. Maintains portfolio dashboard and reporting infrastructure, facilitates quarterly and annual planning cycles, ensures consistent use of templates and tools, identifies portfolio-level risks and opportunities, supports Product Managers with frameworks, and drives continuous improvement of the operating model.[16]

**Squad Members:** Deliver high-quality solutions, provide technical input on feasibility and trade-offs, and collaborate on discovery. Attend squad ceremonies, communicate blockers and risks proactively, maintain technical excellence, collaborate on technical standards, and contribute to continuous improvement.[17]

## Strategic Cascade

Work flows from company strategy down through domains to individual squads:[18]

- **Company Strategy** (3-5 years): Mission, Vision, Strategic Pillars (Executive Team)
- **Company OKRs** (Annual/Quarterly): Objectives & Key Results (Leadership Team)
- **Domain Strategy** (12-24 months): Domain Vision & OKRs (Squad Leads)
- **Product Vision** (12-18 months): Product Strategy & Roadmap (Product Managers)
- **Squad Execution** (Sprint/Quarterly): Features, Stories, Tasks (Squad)

Company OKRs provide the strategic foundation that cascades down to domain strategies and product roadmaps. All product initiatives should trace back to at least one company-level objective, ensuring alignment between execution and strategic priorities.[19]

## Work Intake & Backlog Management

Work enters the product backlog through multiple channels: strategic initiatives from company or domain OKRs, customer feedback, market opportunities, technical debt, bugs and issues, and stakeholder requests.[20]

Product Managers maintain their backlog using a tiered structure:[21]

- **Now (0-3 months):** Committed items, fully refined, ready for development
- **Next (3-6 months):** Prioritized items, discovery in progress, partially refined
- **Later (6-12 months):** Potential items, aligned to vision, minimal detail
- **Icebox (12+ months or unplanned):** Ideas captured but not prioritized

The intake process includes request submission via standardized form, initial triage by Product Manager, backlog placement with appropriate tags and metadata, and communication to requester of status and next steps.[22]

## Governance Ceremonies

**Squad Delivery Ceremonies** (Daily standup, bi-weekly sprint planning/review/retrospective, weekly backlog refinement) align on priorities, surface blockers, define sprint goals, demo completed work, inspect process improvements, and keep backlog ready.[23]

**Cross-Team Governance** (Weekly PM sync, weekly cross-squad dependency standup as needed) aligns Product Managers on roadmap priorities and surfaces cross-squad blockers.[24]

**Portfolio Governance** (Monthly portfolio review, monthly all-hands product demo) reviews portfolio health, makes go/no-go decisions, and creates company-wide visibility.[25]

**Quarterly Planning Governance** (Strategic input review, quarterly product planning, quarterly commitment review) aligns leadership priorities, drafts roadmaps, and finalizes quarterly commitments.[26]

## Change Management

Scope changes are managed by effort threshold: small changes (<10% effort) are adjusted by Product Manager with stakeholder notification; medium changes (10-30% effort) require Squad Lead approval; large changes (>30% effort) require escalation to portfolio review and leadership approval.[27]

Priority changes require Product Manager assessment using the prioritization framework, Squad Lead approval if displacing committed work, stakeholder notification, and portfolio dashboard updates.[28]

Resource changes require Squad Lead assessment of impact, Product Manager roadmap adjustments, portfolio review for reprioritization if necessary, and stakeholder communication.[29]

## Tools & Technology Infrastructure

The core tool stack includes ClickUp for project management and portfolio visibility, Slack for team communication, Google Drive for documentation, Figma for design collaboration, and Github for code repository and CI/CD.[30]

ClickUp serves as the single source of truth with a five-level hierarchy: Workspace (company-level container), Spaces (by domain/function), Folders (by squad/product), Lists (by work type or sprint), and Tasks (discrete units of work).[31]

Dashboards provide real-time visibility: Squad Dashboard (sprint burndown, velocity, cycle time), Product Manager Dashboard (roadmap progress, initiative health, capacity), Portfolio Dashboard (cross-squad metrics, strategic initiative status, resource allocation), and Executive Dashboard (OKR progress, portfolio health, delivery predictability).[32]

## Continuous Improvement

The operating model evolves through structured feedback loops: monthly operating model retrospectives with Product Managers, quarterly operating model reviews by leadership team, and annual operating model audits with external assessment and benchmarking against industry best practices.[33]

## RACI Matrix

A comprehensive RACI matrix clarifies accountability across key activities including product vision and strategy, roadmap definition, customer outcome definition, backlog prioritization, sprint planning, feature definition, solution design and architecture, technical implementation, data model design, cross-squad dependency management, initiative status reporting, portfolio prioritization, resource allocation, go-to-market strategy, success metrics definition, and quarterly business reviews.[34]

## Escalation Paths

Product-level escalations flow from squad-internal discussion (Level 1) to Product Manager decision (Level 2) to Squad Lead involvement (Level 3) to leadership team (Level 4). Portfolio-level escalations flow from Program Manager coordination (Level 1) to PMO facilitation (Level 2) to Squad Leads council (Level 3) to leadership team (Level 4). Escalation is warranted when decisions exceed authority level, cross-functional conflicts cannot be resolved, resource constraints threaten deliverables, strategic misalignment is identified, external dependencies are blocked, or risk threshold is exceeded.[35]
